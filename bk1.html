chapter1

Economics is the study of how individuals, businesses, and governments make decisions about the allocation of scarce resources. It is divided into microeconomics, which focuses on individual and business decisions, and macroeconomics, which looks at the economy as a whole. The laws of supply and demand are the foundation of economic theory, where supply refers to how much of a good or service is available, and demand refers to how much of it people want. Prices tend to stabilize at a point where supply equals demand, known as equilibrium. Different market structures include perfect competition, monopolistic competition, oligopoly, and monopoly, which are determined by the number of sellers, the type of products, and the ease of entry and exit from the market. There are various economic systems: capitalism, which is market-driven; socialism, which is government-driven; and mixed economies, which blend both. Each system has different approaches to resource allocation and production.

chapter2

Gross Domestic Product (GDP) measures the total value of goods and services produced in a country and is used to gauge the size and health of an economy. Real GDP accounts for inflation, while nominal GDP does not. The unemployment rate indicates the percentage of the labor force that is jobless and actively seeking employment, with types of unemployment including frictional, structural, and cyclical. Inflation is the rate at which the general level of prices for goods and services rises, eroding purchasing power, while deflation is the opposite, where prices decrease and can lead to reduced economic activity. Governments use fiscal policy (taxing and spending) and central banks use monetary policy (controlling the money supply and interest rates) to influence economic conditions

chapter3

International trade allows countries to specialize in the production of goods where they have a comparative advantage, leading to greater efficiency and higher standards of living. However, trade barriers such as tariffs, quotas, and subsidies are tools that countries use to protect their domestic industries, which can lead to trade wars and reduce the overall benefits of trade. Exchange rates determine how much one country's currency is worth in terms of another's, influenced by factors such as interest rates, inflation, and economic stability. The balance of payments is a record of all economic transactions between residents of a country and the rest of the world, including the trade balance, foreign investments, and transfers.

chapter4

Economic growth is driven by factors such as capital accumulation, labor force growth, technological advancements, and improvements in productivity. Economic development is broader than economic growth, encompassing improvements in living standards, reduction in poverty, and access to healthcare and education. Developing countries face challenges such as political instability, lack of infrastructure, and limited access to education and healthcare, which impede growth and development. Sustainable development seeks to balance economic growth with environmental protection and social equity, ensuring that future generations can meet their needs
